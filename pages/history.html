The History

Rise of the
Ticketmaster
Monopoly

Scroll down to follow the timeline from Ticketmaster's founding in 1976 to the 2026 DOJ settlement. Each event appears as you reach it.
1976

Ticketmaster Founded

Ticketmaster (TM) is founded and begins selling ticketing hardware to venues.

1985

Computerized Ticketing

Ticketmaster switches to computerized ticketing, modernizing the industry.

2000s

Monopoly Expansion

Ticketmaster acquires competitors and begins to dominate the industry by controlling promotion, venues, and ticketing.

2010

TM + Live Nation Merger

Ticketmaster merges with event promoter company Live Nation, becoming Live Nation Entertainment (LNE). The combination of primary ticketing, live event venues, and artist promotion results in an unprecedented vertical monopoly.

controls 70% primary ticketing owns secondary marketplaces pressures artists & venues hidden fees (78% of base price)
2022

Taylor Swift Eras Tour Disaster

The Taylor Swift Eras Tour presale disaster brings to light fraud, website crashes, and bot-driven scalping, causing massive public outrage. The failure puts Live Nation's monopolistic practices under a national spotlight.

fraud presale crashes public outrage
2023

U.S. Senate Hearing

The U.S. Senate holds a hearing on LNE's monopoly and its harm to artists and customers. Musician/artist Clyde Lawrence testifies about the damaging effects on independent musicians and fans alike.

2024

DOJ Antitrust Lawsuit

The Department of Justice files an antitrust lawsuit against Live Nation Entertainment for monopolizing the live entertainment market, with the ultimate goal of breaking up monopoly.

2025

Artist Pushback

Olivia Dean demands a cap on resale prices for her "The Art of Loving" tour and refunds for fans. Live Nation complies, though this only addresses scalpers/bots, still leaving expensive service charges and hidden fees.

"You are providing a disgusting service, the prices at which you're allowing tickets to be re-sold is vile and completely against our wishes, live music should be affordable and accessible and we need to find a new way of making that possible. BE BETTER."

2026

DOJ Settlement — Not a Full Break-Up

The DOJ reaches a settlement with LNE, with certain conditions attached. However, this is not a full break-up of the company, and falls short of meaningful structural change. If approved, Live Nation will be forced to allow touring artists to hire other promoters, and allow businesses to use other vendors to sell, while paying $280m in damages to nearly 40 states.

partial remedy only
